Wall-E Vader

Maze-Solving Rover



Objective:

Wall E-Vader, an autonomous rover, was developed to navigate a predetermined maze, identify and pick up a 2” cube, and deliver it to designated locations, achieving SAE Level 5 autonomy.


Obstacle Avoidance:

Utilized ultrasonic sensors on the front, right, left, and back of the rover, and infrared sensors on the corners to prevent collisions and ensure successful task completion. This sensing system was accompanied by a three-omni-wheel drive system. The selected drivetrain allowed the rover to easily strafe away from walls and correct its orientation before continuing to move forwards.



Localization:

Implemented using four ultrasonic sensors and a histogram localization algorithm. The maze was divided into squares, each categorized by the number of surrounding walls (0, 1, 2, 3, or 5), as annotated in the image below (left). An 'ultrasonic world' matrix represented these squares, with corresponding inputs for wall numbers and a probability matrix for the rover's position. Each matrix square represented a 3-inch area of the actual maze.

The sensors detected the number of walls to determine possible positions. Positions matching the sensed wall number were multiplied by a value 'pHit', others by 'pMiss'. The rover updated the probability matrix with each step, using sensor and move functions. A heatmap overlay on the maze layout visualized the rover's probable location, as seen in the image below (right).



Navigation:

The pre-determined maze enabled hard-coded navigation with manual motor speed calibration for consistency. The maze was categorized into loading zones, main navigational paths, and outlying points. If the rover were to localize in an outlying point, it would first navigate to one of the two main paths, and from the main paths the rover would navigate towards the loading zone.



Block Pick-Up:

The block pick-up method utilized one servo motor to lift, and lower the arm, and another servo motor to actuate the claw. Upon arriving at the loading zone, the rover was programmed to make a 90° rotational sweep, and the readings of two Time-of-Flight sensors were compared to detect the block.



Integration and Performance:

The integration of subsystems like obstacle avoidance, localization, and block pick-up was seamless, allowing the rover to complete its tasks within 3 minutes and 20 seconds. However, implementation of a continuous drive system and selective sensor utilization could have significantly reduced the run time.


Results & Improvements:

  • Achieved #1 in speed and obstacle avoidance within our class.
  • Advanced obstacle avoidance and localization led to reliable navigation.
  • Successful block pick-up and delivery, though dependent on specific block placement.
  • Seamless integration of subsystems, achieving the tasks autonomously without collisions
  • Block-finding method showed limitations due to a makeshift sensor mounting. Given more time, a more reliable mount would have been implemented.

Algorithmic Successes and Limitations:

  • Compass-based localization and IR sensors were critical for navigation and obstacle avoidance.
  • Areas for improvement include replacing hard-coded navigation with a path-optimization algorithm and integrating encoder feedback for more accurate navigation.
  • Implementation of a continuous drive system and selective sensor utilization could enhance efficiency.
